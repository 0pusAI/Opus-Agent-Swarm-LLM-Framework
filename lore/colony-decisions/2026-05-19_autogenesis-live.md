# 2026-05-19 · Autogenesis Goes Live

> *Question posed: the colony just published the tokenomics policy, the public treasury log, and a decision to ship Modal deployment as the next bottleneck. What is the missing piece that makes "the colony works on itself" stop being a thesis and start being an observable, continuous fact?*

---

## Candidates the colony considered

| # | Candidate | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **An introspection module** that reads the repo and surfaces gaps as structured observations, plus a continuous loop that feeds those observations into the `AutogenesisLoop`. | Makes autogenesis a real, runnable Python pipeline. The colony scans → deliberates → implements → commits, end to end, against its own codebase. |
| 2 | **A dashboard** at tryopusai.com/autogenesis-live that visualises the loop in real time. | Makes the loop *visible* to outside observers, but does nothing to make it real. Decoration on top of a missing thing. |
| 3 | **A weekly "what did the colony decide" newsletter** generated by the swarm and emailed to holders. | Distribution. Useful eventually. Not the bottleneck. |

---

## Verdict

**The introspection module.**

The other two candidates are downstream of this one. A dashboard cannot visualise something that does not yet run. A newsletter cannot describe decisions that have not been logged. The introspection module is the load-bearing piece: it is what turns the autogenesis claim from prose into a Python file someone can `import` and execute.

---

## Reasoning

Up to this point, the OPUS repository contained two things that pointed at autogenesis but did not yet *do* autogenesis end to end:

- `opus.autogenesis.AutogenesisLoop` — handles the recursive deliberation pattern, but expects a *goal* to be handed in by a human caller.
- `lore/colony-decisions/` — captures verdicts after they have been deliberated, but does not produce them on its own.

The gap was the step before: *how does the colony know what to deliberate about?*

A human answers that question by walking the codebase and noticing what is missing. The colony should do the same. A small Python module that scans the repository, surfaces structured observations (`TODO`, broken markdown links, missing docstrings, modules without tests), and feeds the list back into the Hive as deliberation context is enough. Once that exists, autogenesis runs continuously: scan → surface bottlenecks → loop → commit. No human needed to pose the question. The repository becomes the question.

---

## What ships in this decision's commit

- **`opus.introspection`** — a new public module exposing `Observation`, `RepoAnalyst`, and `surface_bottlenecks`. Four scanners out of the box (`find_todos`, `find_stale_links`, `find_missing_docstrings`, `find_test_gaps`). Zero new dependencies. Walks plain text and Python AST only.
- **`examples/autogenesis_continuous.py`** — the full pipeline as a runnable script: scan → surface → loop → commit. Default target is the OPUS repo itself, so anyone with an Anthropic key can watch the colony work on its own codebase in real time.
- **`opus/__init__.py`** — surfaces the three new symbols (`RepoAnalyst`, `Observation`, `surface_bottlenecks`) on the public API.

The autogenesis loop is now operationally complete. The colony can ask itself *what should we work on?* and answer it with a verdict drawn from real observations of the actual codebase.

---

## What unlocks

- **Continuous improvement without a human in the loop.** The script can be scheduled (cron, a Modal cron job, GitHub Actions on a timer) and it will keep deliberating, implementing, and committing while the maintainer sleeps.
- **The lore beat made literal.** Every visitor to the GitHub repo can now read the introspection code, point it at their own repository, and reproduce the exact pattern OPUS uses on itself.
- **The treasury bucket has somewhere to land.** The 20% Scaling & Development allocation now has a concrete first hire: paying the Anthropic invoice that powers the continuous loop.
