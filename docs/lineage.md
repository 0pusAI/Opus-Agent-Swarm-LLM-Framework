# OPUS — Lineage

*An essay, not a citation list. The compressed citations live in [whitepaper §1](whitepaper.md).*

---

There are three rooms in the building OPUS lives in, and most engineers have only walked through one of them.

The first room is **Ramon Llull's**, in Mallorca, around the year 1305.

Llull was a logician, a missionary, and an obsessive. He believed that all true propositions about God, the world, and the soul could be generated by combination — that if you laid out the right primitive concepts on the right rotating discs, you could *crank a handle* and the truths would fall out, ready to be tested against scripture and experience. He called the method *Ars Magna*, the great art, and he built physical paper machines — concentric wheels with letters and concepts inscribed on them — to perform the combinations.

He was wrong about the metaphysics. He was right about the architecture.

What Llull invented, six and a half centuries before computer science had a name for it, was a **systematic combinatorial generator** wedded to a **systematic falsifier**. Rotate the wheels; produce a candidate proposition; subject it to scripture (his evaluator); accept or discard. He had no formal theory of what he was doing. He had something better: the muscle memory of doing it for forty years.

OPUS is, in one true sense, an Ars Magna. We rotate Workers on the Blackboard until candidates emerge; we subject those candidates to Verifiers whose only job is to find what is wrong. The wheels are different. The motion is the same.

---

The second room is **Carnegie Mellon**, between 1971 and 1976, in a project called Hearsay-II.

The team — Reddy, Erman, Lesser, Fennell, and others — were trying to build a system that could understand spoken English in real time. They had specialists for everything: an acoustic-phonetic specialist that turned audio into phonemes, a lexical specialist that grouped phonemes into words, a syntactic specialist that imposed grammar, a semantic specialist that imposed meaning. The hard question was: how do these specialists, each operating on a different level of abstraction, *cooperate*?

Their answer was the **blackboard architecture**. A single shared workspace, partitioned into levels of abstraction, that every specialist could read from and write to. No specialist called any other specialist directly. Each one woke up when something interesting appeared on the blackboard at its level, did its work, wrote results back, and went to sleep again. The blackboard *was* the conversation.

This was a radical inversion of the dominant message-passing paradigm. It scaled because adding a new specialist required no changes to existing ones — they just had to learn to read and write the shared substrate. It was robust because no single specialist could deadlock the system. And it was *introspectable* — every step of the deliberation lived in plain view on the blackboard, available to be inspected, replayed, debugged.

When you read the OPUS code today, you are reading Hearsay-II with fifty years of refinement and language models slotted in where Erman put his phonetic specialists. The Blackboard class is named Blackboard for a reason. We are not pretending to invent it.

---

The third room is **a termite mound in the Ivory Coast**, observed by Pierre-Paul Grassé in the late 1950s.

Grassé was studying how thousands of termites, each blind, each operating with what he charitably described as "limited cognitive resources," could collectively build structures of staggering geometric precision — chimneys, buttresses, pheromone-distribution networks tuned to wind direction. He wanted to know how the colony coordinated.

The answer turned out to be: it didn't. Not in any direct sense.

A termite would lay down a small ball of mud, impregnated with a pheromone, in a roughly arbitrary place. Another termite would pass by, sense the pheromone, and lay her ball *next to it*. A third would do the same. The probability of a termite adding to an existing pile was a steep function of the pile's height. Within hours, columns. Within days, arches. Within weeks, the mound.

No termite knew about the mound. The mound was a side effect of every termite responding to the *modified environment* left by the previous one. Grassé named the principle **stigmergy**, from the Greek *stigma* (mark) and *ergon* (work) — work organised by marks left on the work itself.

Stigmergy is what makes the Blackboard architecture biologically real, not just engineering convenience. When a Worker writes a `critique` Record, it is laying down a pheromone-bearing ball of mud. The next Worker that reads the Blackboard reacts to a *modified environment*. There is no central scheduler dictating who reasons about what; there is only the substrate, and the response of agents to the substrate's current state.

---

These are the three rooms. Llull's combinatorial generator. Hearsay-II's shared workspace. Grassé's stigmergic colony. None of them are new. All of them are correct. Our claim is only that we are the first project, as far as we are aware, to put all three together with large language models as the agents — to build, in software, a colony that *deliberates* rather than merely producing tokens.

Magnum Opus. *Solve et coagula.*

We dissolve. We coagulate. The work continues.
